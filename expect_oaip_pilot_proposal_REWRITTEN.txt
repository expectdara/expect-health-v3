Executive Summary

Problem Statement: Pelvic floor symptoms are widespread, costly, and treatable with low-risk first-line training--but delivering correct, consistent coaching does not scale with current clinician capacity. AI is built to scale that supervision.

Prevalence: Urinary incontinence affects ~47%--63% of U.S. women. In Utah, a high-volume maternity state, ~1 in 3 women experience incontinence after childbirth. (Milsom et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2021; Patel et al., 2022; Che et al., 2025; Thom & Rortveit, 2010)

Clinician Scarcity: Utah has only 7 board-certified women's health/pelvic floor physical therapists for approximately 1.6 million adult female residents. (American Board of Physical Therapy Specialties; U.S. Census)

Harms of Inaction: Incontinence is directly linked to social isolation, reduced quality of life, reduced physical activity, and a higher risk of postpartum depression. (Milsom et al., 2019; Gallego-Gomez et al., 2024)

Low-Risk Intervention: Pelvic floor physical therapy is conservative but effective, with bounded adverse events--unlike pharmaceutical interventions.

Proposed Solution: Expect uses AI to extend PT-grade pelvic floor care to underserved populations.


1. About You

1.1 What Is Your Product?

Expect is an AI-augmented pelvic floor physical therapy (PF-PT) platform that delivers clinically validated assessment, AI-generated treatment planning, and ongoing care management to women experiencing pelvic floor dysfunction--primarily urinary incontinence, pelvic pain, and related postpartum conditions.

The platform uses deterministic scoring algorithms and rule-based plan generation to analyze patient responses across a custom clinical battery using the full ICIQ-UI SF plus selected FLUTS Filling/Voiding items, ICIQ-FLUTSsex, GUPI-Female, PHQ-2, Bristol Stool Scale, and POPDI-6. It computes severity scores and diagnostic classifications, then generates an individualized treatment plan including ICD-10 diagnoses, exercise prescriptions, adjunct device/behavioral recommendations, precautions, and progression criteria.

Expect follows a graduated autonomy model, consistent with the approved Doctronic pilot. During the initial calibration phase, 100% of AI-augmented plans are reviewed, edited, and approved by a licensed Physical Therapist before any treatment recommendation reaches the patient. As the AI demonstrates statistical reliability across 250+ cases per diagnosis subtype, low-risk plans may be auto-approved with ongoing 10% human audit. High-risk, complex, and first-time patients always receive full PT review. The PT retains full authority to modify, reject, or augment any AI output at any phase. This "earned autonomy" model ensures AI augments the clinician, never replaces them--consistent with the Utah legislature's "doctor, not device" principle.

Expect has active partnerships across government levels: a Cooperative Research and Development Agreement (CRADA) with the Veterans Health Administration (VHA), an IRB-approved study with the University of Alabama, and a contract with the Boston Public Health Commission. Expect is also a member of the Advanced Medical Technology Association (AdvaMed).

1.2 Who Does Your Product Serve?

The primary population served is women in Utah experiencing:
- Postpartum pelvic floor dysfunction (stress incontinence, urgency, pelvic organ prolapse symptoms)
- Chronic pelvic pain (vulvodynia, dyspareunia, interstitial cystitis symptoms)
- Urinary incontinence (Stress, urge, or mixed--affecting an estimated 47%-63% of women in the United States).
- Pelvic floor dysfunction related to aging (menopausal changes, deconditioning)

The platform specifically targets underserved populations:
- Utah Medicaid beneficiaries with limited access to specialist pelvic floor PT (PF-PT)
- Rural Utah residents more than 60 minutes from the nearest PF-PT provider
- Postpartum women within the first year after delivery, when PF symptoms are most treatable
- Women who avoid in-person PF-PT due to stigma, childcare barriers, or cost

1.3 Benefits to Utahns

Access expansion: Utah has 7 PF-PT specialists for 1.6 million residents. The platform enables any Utah-licensed PT to deliver evidence-based PF care with AI-augmented clinical decision support, expanding the pool of providers who can treat these conditions. (American Board of Physical Therapy Specialties; U.S. Census)

Addresses silent epidemic: Urinary incontinence affects over half of U.S. women, yet most go untreated due to stigma and access barriers.

Maternal health outcomes: Utah has among the highest birth rates in the nation. Approximately one-third of women experience urinary incontinence in the first year postpartum, and the prevalence after vaginal birth is about twice that after cesarean delivery, as shown in a systematic review published in the British Journal of Obstetrics and Gynaecology. Early intervention with PF-PT reduces long-term incontinence, pain, and surgical intervention rates.

Cost reduction: Asynchronous delivery eliminates barriers of in-person visits (travel, childcare, time off work) and the scheduling constraints of synchronous telehealth. It allows women with irregular schedules or limited privacy to engage in care. AI-assisted intake is projected to reduce PT administrative burden by approximately 60% based on preliminary internal time-motion analysis (formal validation planned during Phase 1), enabling service to rural populations where provider ratios are unfavorable.

Medicaid efficiency: Conservative treatment of PF dysfunction via PT ($2,000-4,000 per course) costs a fraction of surgical intervention ($15,000-40,000). AI augmentation further reduces per-patient cost.

Healthcare access fairness: Rural Utah residents and Medicaid beneficiaries gain access to specialist-level PF-PT that was previously available only in urban centers.

Regulatory innovation data: The pilot generates real-world evidence on AI safety, efficacy, and patient experience in a controlled healthcare setting, informing future Utah AI policy across all healthcare domains.

Federal partnership leverage: The VHA CRADA (see Section 1.1) may extend validated pilot findings to the VA's 9 million enrolled veterans.

1.4 End-to-End Customer Experience

Patient Journey

AI Disclosure at Every Touchpoint

Consistent with the Utah AI Policy Act and OAIP expectations, disclosure of AI utilization begins at the very first touchpoint (recruitment ad, landing page, intake page), not just in the consent form. Every patient-facing touchpoint includes: "This service utilizes AI to assist licensed professionals in care planning. All treatment decisions are finalized by a human clinician."

Patient Journey Overview

The patient journey proceeds through eight steps, with clear handoffs between patient, AI, and clinician:

Step 1: Email Collection (Landing Page)
Patient provides email address only. Low friction. Enables abandonment recovery (non-PHI reminder emails if they drop off mid-assessment). AI disclosure is visible on this page.

Step 2: Identity Verification, Informed Consent & Safety/Eligibility Screening
The patient completes email-based identity verification: a 6-digit one-time code is sent to the email address provided at registration, and the patient must enter the code to proceed. This confirms ownership of the email address and enables HIPAA-compliant communication. If additional identity assurance is required by the OAIP during the pilot, Expect will implement HIPAA-compliant photo ID verification via Persona (withpersona.com), a SOC 2 Type II certified identity verification provider that executes BAAs. However, given that a licensed PT reviews 100% of plans in Phase 1 and that patients attest to Utah residency in the informed consent, email verification provides a proportionate level of identity assurance without introducing friction that could reduce access for underserved populations. The patient then receives a clear, plain-language informed consent form (10 required checkboxes). The form discloses that AI is used in their care, that a licensed PT reviews all recommendations (during Phase 1), that they may opt out of AI-assisted care at any time without losing access to PT services, and that Expect will provide corrective care if an AI-supported workflow issue causes harm.

Upon consent, the patient completes:
- Safety screening (5 red-flag questions): Fever, bleeding, chest pain, headache with vision changes, UTI symptoms. Positive screens redirect to 911 or physician immediately--no exercise plan is generated. Any attempt to subsequently change a positive safety answer triggers a confirmation modal, and the change is logged as a SAFETY_ANSWER_CHANGED event for PT review and OAIP oversight.
- Eligibility screening (6 exclusion questions, plus 1 conditional for high-risk pregnancy): Positive screens redirect to physician/specialist; the AI does not generate plans for excluded conditions. See Section 3.8 for the full exclusionary diagnosis list and prenatal protocol.
- Depression screening (PHQ-2): Two-question validated screen for depression. Positive scores (>=3 out of 6) trigger an inline clinical consistency warning and flag the case for the reviewing PT to verify capacity to consent. The depression flag is logged as a depression_screen_positive audit event with severity classification (MODERATE for scores 3-4, HIGH for scores 5-6) and surfaced in the OAIP Red Flags dashboard.

Step 3: Clinical Intake Assessment
The patient completes a guided assessment across 10 intake steps comprising demographics (including pregnancy status, delivery date for postpartum patients, and delivery type--if "Currently pregnant" is selected, a PRENATAL_FLAG is set and a gentle inline note appears: "We'll tailor your care plan with prenatal-safe modifications so you can safely support your pelvic floor throughout your pregnancy"), provider selection via NPI Registry lookup (verified against the CMS National Provider Identifier database with typo-tolerant matching), and a custom clinical battery built from validated instruments:
- ICIQ-UI SF (4 questions): Bladder leakage severity and subtyping (full standard instrument)
- Selected FLUTS Filling/Voiding items (12 questions): Nocturia, urgency, daytime frequency (Filling subscale) and hesitancy, straining, intermittency (Voiding subscale) with bother scores. These are selected items from the ICIQ-FLUTS; the FLUTS Incontinence subscale is omitted because the ICIQ-UI SF already captures incontinence severity and subtype
- ICIQ-FLUTSsex (8 questions): Sexual health impact (full standard instrument)
- GUPI-Female + Pain Assessment (23 questions): Pain locations, frequency, severity, functional impact, quality-of-life subscales, symptom triggers, and bladder pain questions. Pain intensity is assessed once using a single NRS scale and applied to both the GUPI pain subscale and the standalone pain composite score (measure-once-use-twice design), reducing patient burden while maintaining clinical validity across both instruments
- PHQ-2 (2 questions): Validated two-item depression screen (threshold >=3/6)
- Bristol Stool Scale + bowel function (3 questions): Stool form, straining frequency, bowel frequency
- POPDI-6 (6 questions): Pelvic Organ Prolapse Distress Inventory, short form. Yes/no with conditional bother scale for symptom-based prolapse screening

The patient also answers questions about caffeine/alcohol/water intake, bowel function, symptom triggers, activity avoidance, current medications, prior treatment history, cueing preference, and personal goals. Throughout the intake, a real-time consistency checker (9 cross-instrument rules) detects contradictory answers and displays inline warnings. Input validation includes DOB age guardrails (18--120) and auto-formatted phone numbers.

Provider selection uses three pathways: (1) NPI Registry lookup (auto-verified), (2) demo/sandbox providers (for testing), and (3) concierge verification for providers not in the NPI Registry. Concierge-submitted providers are held in "Pending Verification"--no fax is sent until verified. See Step 7.

Step 4: Account Creation (Pre-Submission)
After the last question and before submission, the patient must: confirm their email, create a password, and check a consent box: "I consent to receive electronic notifications about my care plan. I understand these notifications will link to a secure portal and will not contain sensitive health details directly."

This ensures HIPAA-compliant electronic communication and prevents accidental PHI-adjacent notifications to wrong recipients via email verification.

Step 5: AI Processing
The AI processes the intake in six steps:
1. Computes validated scores (ICIQ 0--21, FLUTS F/V subscales, GUPI 0--45, pain composite 0--10)
2. Classifies incontinence subtype (stress/urge/mixed/continuous) and assigns a severity tier (Beginner/Moderate/Advanced per Klovning severity bands)
3. Generates ICD-10 diagnoses, including pudendal neuralgia detection when sitting-related pain triggers are present with composite pain >6/10
4. Risk-stratifies the patient (green/yellow)
5. Produces a draft treatment plan with personalized exercises (cueing per Crane & Dugan 2025), adjuncts, precautions, and progression criteria
6. If the patient has an active PRENATAL_FLAG, applies prenatal exercise substitutions (see Section 3.8, Prenatal Pelvic Floor Protocol).

The AI functions as a clinical drafting tool. It does not make autonomous clinical decisions.

Step 6: PT Review (Phase 1: 100% of Plans)
A Utah-licensed PT receives the complete intake in their provider dashboard, including all raw patient responses (expandable by section with scoring verification), computed scores, AI-generated diagnoses, eligibility screening results, PHQ-2 score, and the full treatment plan. The PT can edit every element: goals, exercises (name/sets/reps/hold/frequency), adjunct recommendations, precautions, and progression criteria.

Pre-approval guardrails warn the PT if: review time is under 2 minutes, safety or eligibility flags were positive, a safety answer was changed during intake, PHQ-2 is positive, pudendal neuralgia indicators are present, the plan seems inconsistent with scores, modifications lack documented rationale, or the patient has an active prenatal protocol. The PT generates an editable encounter note (CMS-compliant SOAP format) with AI-assisted shorthand expansion (e.g., "PFM" auto-expands to "pelvic floor muscle"), reviews/edits it, and approves the plan.

The PT review interface also displays the provider routing destination with fax verification status: VERIFIED (auto-populated from NPI Registry), DEFAULT (pre-populated from organization/specialty matching rules when NPI Registry has no fax on file), or MANUAL ENTRY (entered by the PT). Default fax usage is logged as a DEFAULT_FAX_APPLIED audit event and surfaced as a yellow flag in the OAIP Red Flags dashboard.

In Phase 2+, low-complexity cases may receive expedited review or auto-approval with audit. High-complexity cases always receive full PT review. See Section 3.1 for phase details.

Step 7: Notification & Distribution
Upon approval, the encounter note is transmitted to the referring physician via HIPAA-compliant fax. The fax workflow supports three verification states:

1. NPI-Verified fax: Auto-populated from the CMS NPI Registry. Displayed with a green "VERIFIED" badge.
2. Default fax: When the NPI Registry has no fax number on file, the system pre-populates a default fax number based on the provider's organization and specialty using predefined rules for major Utah health systems (University of Utah and Intermountain Health, by OB/GYN and PCP/IM/FM specialty). Displayed with an amber "DEFAULT" badge and a "Default -- verify before sending" warning. A DEFAULT_FAX_APPLIED audit event is logged with the provider, NPI, fax number used, and matching rule.
3. Manual entry: The PT may manually enter or edit any fax number. Displayed with an amber "MANUAL ENTRY" warning.

If the provider was submitted through the concierge verification flow and verification is still pending, fax transmission is held: the PT sees a "HOLD -- Pending Destination Verification" banner, and the Send Fax button is not available until the provider is verified.

The patient receives a HIPAA-compliant notification email: "You have a new document available in your secure Expect portal." No PHI appears in the email. The patient logs into the portal to view their full care plan.

Step 8: Care Plan Delivery & Ongoing Care
The patient views their personalized care plan in the secure portal across five tabs: Scores (assessment results with severity explanations), Exercises (detailed instructions with personalized cueing, common mistakes, stop conditions), Recommendations (adjunct devices and behavioral programs), Strategies (bladder retraining, The Knack, daily habits, timeline expectations), and Full Report (printable clinical summary with PT attestation).

The patient can export their care plan for sharing with their primary care provider through a dedicated "Export for MyChart / EHR" button. This opens a modal titled "Share with your Doctor" with step-by-step instructions: (1) download a secure PDF, (2) log in to their health system's patient portal (e.g., Intermountain MyChart), and (3) attach the file via Messages or Document Sharing. The PDF is generated entirely client-side--no PHI is transmitted externally. A CARE_PLAN_DOWNLOADED audit event is logged with context PATIENT_DIRECTED_EHR_SHARING.

The patient follows the home exercise program, reports symptoms via mid-treatment check-ins, and communicates with their PT through secure messaging. The PT monitors adherence, adjusts the plan as needed, and documents outcomes. The platform tracks ICIQ and pain score trends over time.

Patients may report concerns at any time through a persistent "Report a Problem" button embedded in their care plan portal, which provides six reporting pathways:

1. Pain during exercises: Severity slider (0-10). Logged as EXERCISE_PAIN_REPORT.
2. Worsening symptoms: Severity slider (0-10), new symptom vs. getting worse toggle, free-text description. Logged as CLINICAL_REGRESSION_FLAG with severity-based triage (CRITICAL for severity >=7, HIGH for >=4, MODERATE for <4).
3. Adverse event: Categorized by type (new symptom, bleeding, adverse reaction, other) with detailed description. Logged as adverse_event_report.
4. Instruction clarity: Free-text question about care plan instructions. Logged as clinical_review_request.
5. AI opt-out: Request to opt out of AI-assisted care with optional context. Logged as clinical_review_request with category "optout."
6. Technical issue: Description of platform problem. Logged as TECHNICAL_ISSUE_REPORT.

All reports are logged in the immutable audit trail and surfaced in the OAIP Red Flags dashboard.

PT Provider Experience
PTs access the provider portal via individual accounts (email + password, with PBKDF2-hashed credentials). Each PT's identity is captured in every audit event, enabling individual accountability and modification tracking. The provider view has three sub-tabs: (1) a Dashboard showing enrolled patients, average adherence, ICIQ/pain score trends, and pending reviews; (2) a Patients tab for reviewing individual cases, accessing full intake data, and managing care plans; and (3) an Audit Log with a de-identification toggle for PHI-safe review. A review timer is prominently displayed at the top of each case for CMS documentation. An immutable audit log records every action with the individual PT's identity for regulatory compliance.

1. Compliance: Phase status, enrollment, adherence, red flag count, live AI-PT agreement rate, and four-category compliance checklist.
2. Red Flags & Alerts: All clinical safety signals (11 flag types) sorted by severity with actionable detail.
3. Follow-Up Schedule: Patient follow-up tracking and overdue alerts.
4. Live Audit Stream: Full event log with Auditor Mode de-identification toggle (see Section 4.3).
5. Interoperability / HIE: FHIR R4 resource preview supporting future integration with UHIN CHIE and Intermountain Health's Care Everywhere network.
6. Outcome Research: De-identified outcome records, tier-level outcomes, AI-PT agreement rate with detailed PT modification diffs, and biomarker discovery signals (see Section 5.3).

Details of each view are described in Appendix A.

The OAIP can access de-identified datasets, monthly/quarterly reports, and the full audit log at any time.

Right to Opt Out
Patients may opt out of AI-assisted care at any time without losing access to PT services. Opt-out is available through the consent form (at enrollment), the "Report a Problem" button in the secure portal (at any time during the program, via the "I want to opt out of AI-assisted care" pathway), or by contacting Expect's designated privacy contact. Upon opt-out, the patient's existing care plan remains available; no new AI-generated recommendations are delivered. The PT may continue to provide non-AI-assisted telehealth care if clinically appropriate. Opt-out rates are tracked and reported to the OAIP as part of the monthly Patient Feedback Report. Opt-out requests are logged as clinical_review_request audit events with category "optout" and surfaced as HIGH-severity flags in the OAIP Red Flags dashboard.

3. Pilot Design

3.1 Duration and Timeline

Requested pilot duration: 12 months, with option for a single 12-month extension per UAIPA.

Enrollment Projections: Expect uses a tiered enrollment model for Phase 1 to set realistic expectations while preserving statistical ambition:

- Minimum Viable: 50 patients -- Sufficient for safety data and primary outcomes analysis (ICIQ improvement >= 3 points). Provides 80% power to detect a 3-point ICIQ change (SD ~4) with paired t-test. Enables initial adverse event rate estimation and PT workflow validation.
- Target: 150 patients -- Powered for secondary outcomes (pain reduction, adherence, PHQ-2 trends) and subgroup analysis by incontinence subtype (Stress, Urge, Mixed). Enables meaningful fairness analysis across demographic groups and preliminary cue-preference response comparison.
- Stretch: 250 patients -- Full statistical power across all endpoints, including multi-instrument biomarker discovery (see Section 5.3). Sufficient for three-way subtype comparison with Bonferroni correction and exploratory predictive modeling. Supports peer-reviewed publication with robust sample size.

Phase 2 targets 500 enrolled, Phase 3 targets 500 enrolled, for a total of approximately 700-1,250 patients over the 12-month pilot depending on Phase 1 tier achieved. Enrollment channels include OB/GYN and PCP referral networks, Utah Medicaid MCO partnerships, and direct-to-consumer outreach. Phase progression is gated by volume (250 consecutive AI-PT matches per subtype at the Stretch tier, or proportionally adjusted at lower tiers), not calendar, so if enrollment is slower than projected, Expect will request the 12-month extension permitted under UAIPA.

Data Retention and Disposition (Days 30--90)

- The OAIP receives a final comprehensive pilot report within 60 days of wind-down initiation, covering all outcomes, safety data, lessons learned, and recommendations.

Platform Disposition
- If the pilot demonstrates safety and efficacy and Expect intends to continue operations in Utah, Expect will apply for full regulatory approval or an RMA extension before the wind-down period ends.
- If the pilot does not continue, the platform is decommissioned after the data retention obligations are fulfilled. Source code and algorithm documentation are preserved for audit purposes.

3.7 Alignment with Approved Utah Sandbox Precedents

Expect's pilot design mirrors the Doctronic pilot--the first AI healthcare application approved under the Utah AI Regulatory Sandbox--across four dimensions:
- Volume-Based Gating: Tiered enrollment targets (Minimum Viable: 50, Target: 150, Stretch: 250) per diagnosis category (Stress UI, Urge UI, Mixed UI) prior to any AI autonomy, ensuring statistically meaningful validation rather than calendar-based phase advancement. At all tiers, 100% PT review is maintained until phase progression criteria are met.
- Liability Parity: Malpractice insurance covers the AI algorithm as a distinct provider, holding the AI to the same liability standard as a licensed clinician.
- 10% Post-Calibration Audit Rate: After the calibration period (minimum 50 cases per subtype), 10% human audit rate for low-risk cases.
- Identity Verification: Email-based verification at intake with HIPAA-compliant photo ID verification (via Persona) available if OAIP requires additional identity assurance.

Lower Risk Profile Than Approved Precedent: Doctronic's approved pilot authorizes AI to dispense medications--which carry risks of drug interactions, overdose, and allergic reactions. Expect prescribes pelvic floor exercises. The worst-case outcome is temporary muscle soreness, not a life-threatening adverse event.

Like Doctronic's medication refills, Expect's AI applies a deterministic protocol to validated scores--not open-ended clinical judgment. Severity bands determine exercise tier; ICD-10 codes map to score thresholds. Suspected pudendal neuralgia is flagged for mandatory PT review, never acted on autonomously.

3.8 Exclusionary Diagnosis List ("Safe Harbor" Scope)

To define a strict "Safe Harbor" of low-risk conditions--consistent with Doctronic's exclusionary formulary approach--Expect explicitly excludes the following from AI-generated treatment plans. Any positive screen on an unconditional exclusion question triggers immediate referral to a physician or specialist:
1. Neurogenic bladder or known neurological conditions affecting pelvic floor function (MS, spinal cord injury, Parkinson's, stroke)
2. Post-surgical mesh complications or any post-surgical pelvic condition
3. Grade 3 or higher pelvic organ prolapse
4. Known or suspected pelvic malignancy (bladder, uterine, cervical, ovarian)
5. Active pelvic infection, fistula, or abscess
6. Interstitial cystitis with confirmed Hunner lesions (requires specialized urological management)
7. High-risk pregnancy or medical advice to avoid exercise (conditional; appears only for patients who indicate active pregnancy on the demographics screen). A "yes" blocks plan generation with: "Please consult your prenatal care team before starting any pelvic floor program. We're here when you get the green light."
- Patients under 18 years of age (screened via DOB validation at consent)

Active pregnancy is not an exclusion. Pelvic floor PT during pregnancy is evidence-based and recommended by ACOG and the International Continence Society. Patients with normal pregnancies receive a prenatal-adapted care plan (see Prenatal Pelvic Floor Protocol below).

This "negative scope" approach clarifies what the AI will never attempt, limiting the risk surface to well-defined, exercise-responsive conditions with established standards of care.

Prenatal Pelvic Floor Protocol

Patients who indicate "Currently pregnant" on the demographics screen are not excluded from the platform. Instead, the system applies a prenatal-adapted care pathway:

- PRENATAL_FLAG is set on the patient's record. A gentle inline note appears: "We'll tailor your care plan with prenatal-safe modifications so you can safely support your pelvic floor throughout your pregnancy." This is not a blocker.
- A conditional high-risk screening question is shown during eligibility screening: "Have you been told by your OB/GYN or midwife that you have a high-risk pregnancy or been advised to avoid exercise?" A "yes" blocks plan generation. A "no" allows the patient to proceed.
- The AI plan generator automatically substitutes supine (lying-flat) exercises with incline or side-lying variants to avoid vena cava compression:
  - Supine PF Activation becomes Incline PF Activation (30-45 degree incline with pillows)
  - Bridge + PF becomes Incline Bridge + PF (incline with hip towel roll)
  - Diaphragmatic Breathing + PF becomes Diaphragmatic Breathing + PF (Prenatal) (incline or seated)
  - Diaphragmatic Breathing becomes Diaphragmatic Breathing + PF (Prenatal)
- Kegels (Quick-Flick and Endurance) and Pain De-Sensitization Breathing are position-neutral and remain unchanged.
- Each modified exercise displays a "Prenatal-adapted" badge and an inline explanation: "Modified for pregnancy -- lying flat on your back after the first trimester can compress a major blood vessel. This exercise has been adjusted to a supported incline or side-lying position."
- A precaution block is added: "Your exercises have been adapted for pregnancy. Avoid lying flat on your back -- use a wedge pillow or rolled towel under your right hip for any floor exercises. Stop any exercise that causes discomfort and contact your OB/GYN or midwife."
- The care plan is labeled "Prenatal Pelvic Floor Protocol."
- The reviewing PT receives a clinical alert: "Patient is currently pregnant. Exercise modifications for supine positioning have been automatically applied. Please review for trimester appropriateness."
- A PRENATAL_PROTOCOL_APPLIED flag is surfaced in the OAIP Red Flags dashboard at MODERATE (amber) severity. This is a clinical routing note, not a safety concern.
- Patients route to their selected provider as normal (OB/GYN if selected, PCP otherwise). There is no forced rerouting.

How Exclusions Are Screened

Six plain-language questions are always presented during the eligibility screening step of the clinical intake (Step 2 of the patient journey, Section 1.4), screening for the unconditional exclusions listed above (items 1-6). A seventh question--about high-risk pregnancy--is conditionally shown only to patients who indicate active pregnancy on the demographics screen.

A "yes" to any unconditional exclusion question halts the intake flow and displays a condition-specific message directing the patient to their physician or specialist. No AI-generated plan is created. For the conditional high-risk pregnancy question, a "yes" similarly halts the intake with the message: "Please consult your prenatal care team before starting any pelvic floor program. We're here when you get the green light." A "no" allows the patient to proceed with the prenatal-adapted care plan. Positive exclusion screens are prominently flagged in the PT review dashboard.

These screening questions use patient self-report. As with any clinical screening, false negatives are possible. The PT review layer provides a secondary safety net.

5.3 Proprietary Technology Innovation

Expect's platform includes two novel technology subsystems that go beyond the application of published clinical instruments. These subsystems are the subject of provisional patent filings and represent Expect's core intellectual property.

Adaptive Outcome Algorithm

The platform collects structured, PHI-free outcome records linking each patient's baseline clinical profile (instrument scores, demographic bracket, pregnancy status, weeks postpartum at enrollment, delivery type, prior treatment history, symptom triggers, comorbidity flags) to their treatment parameters (exercise tier, adjunct types, cueing style, PT modifications) and Week 8 outcomes (ICIQ delta, pain delta, adherence rate, patient-reported satisfaction). Each outcome record is de-identified at creation -- age is stored as a bracket (e.g., "30-39"), weeks postpartum is stored as an integer (not a date), and no name, date of birth, or contact information is retained. Weeks postpartum and delivery type enable confound control: outcomes can be stratified to distinguish treatment effect from natural postpartum recovery.

As outcome records accumulate, the algorithm identifies which baseline profile + treatment parameter combinations produce the greatest clinical improvement. In Phase 1, this data is observational only -- the algorithm does not influence plan generation. In Phase 2 and beyond, the outcome data may be used to refine exercise selection, adjunct recommendations, and cueing strategies for patients with similar baseline profiles, creating an adaptive feedback loop that improves over time. This "learning from outcomes" capability is architecturally distinct from the deterministic scoring used for initial plan generation.

PT Modification Tracking and Protocol Refinement

Every PT interaction with an AI-generated plan is tracked as a structured data signal. The platform captures detailed diffs of PT modifications: which exercises were added, removed, or modified (with specific parameter changes such as sets, reps, hold times, and frequency); which adjuncts were added or removed; and which goals were revised. An AI-PT agreement rate is computed in real-time across all approved plans, measuring the percentage of plans the PT approved without substantive modification.

PT modification patterns are tracked longitudinally and reviewed by Dr. Dugan and the clinical team on a regular cadence to identify protocol improvements. For example, if PTs consistently add a specific adjunct for a patient profile the AI missed, the clinical team updates the plan generation rules in a future release. This is a clinician-driven protocol refinement process -- not automated model fine-tuning. No AI model is retrained or updated based on PT edits without explicit clinical review and code-level changes approved by the clinical director.

The adaptive algorithm's key innovation is that it operates entirely on de-identified, structured outcome data -- not on raw clinical text, images, or patient communications. This design enables continuous improvement while maintaining full HIPAA compliance and avoiding the privacy risks associated with large language model training on clinical data.

Multi-Instrument Biomarker Discovery Engine

The platform includes a statistical analysis engine designed to discover novel multi-instrument patterns ("digital biomarkers") that predict treatment response, non-response, or dropout. Five initial biomarker hypotheses are embedded in the platform:

1. Non-Response Predictor: High GUPI + high activity avoidance + PHQ-2 positive may predict poor ICIQ improvement despite treatment adherence.
2. Pudendal Early Detection: FLUTS voiding subscale + pain composite + sitting trigger at lower thresholds than the current clinical heuristic may identify pudendal involvement earlier.
3. Dropout Predictor: High avoidance + elevated PHQ-2 + default cueing + no prior treatment history may predict early program abandonment.
4. Cue-Response Interaction: Breathing-based cueing may produce better outcomes than biologic cueing for patients with high genitourinary pain (GUPI >= 15).
5. Tier Boundary Signal: Patients near ICIQ tier boundaries (scores 5-7 or 12-14) may show differential response patterns that inform more precise tier assignment.

Each hypothesis is evaluated using inline Welch's t-test with Cohen's d effect size calculation, requiring no external statistical libraries. A minimum of 30 completed outcome records is required before any signal analysis is performed. Biomarker signals are classified as "strong" (p < 0.01, |d| >= 0.5), "emerging" (p < 0.05, |d| >= 0.3), or "insufficient data."

The biomarker discovery engine's innovation is the systematic application of cross-instrument correlation analysis to pelvic floor treatment outcomes -- a combination that has not been explored in the published literature. The custom clinical battery used in this platform (ICIQ-UI SF, selected FLUTS Filling/Voiding items, ICIQ-FLUTSsex, GUPI-Female, PHQ-2, Bristol Stool Scale, and POPDI-6) has never been administered together in a structured digital workflow that enables automated pattern discovery across its combined score space.

All biomarker signals are exploratory hypotheses generated from pilot data. Full validation requires IRB-approved analysis with appropriate sample sizes and pre-registered protocols. The OAIP Outcome Research dashboard displays biomarker signals for transparency.

APPENDIX B: Patient Safety, Clinical Oversight, and Remediation Framework

Expect -- Utah OAIP Pilot
February 2026

0. Intervention Risk Profile

Pelvic floor physical therapy is among the lowest-risk interventions in clinical medicine. The Expect pilot delivers exercise recommendations (bodyweight pelvic floor contractions, breathing coordination exercises, bridges) and behavioral guidance (bladder retraining, bowel management, fluid/caffeine management). The intervention:
- Involves no pharmaceuticals. No drugs are prescribed, dispensed, or recommended. No dosing risk, drug interaction risk, or adverse pharmacological reaction risk.
- Involves no implanted or invasive devices. Unlike intravaginal biofeedback devices, Expect's program is instruction-only. No device malfunction or infection risk.
- Involves no surgical or procedural risk. No anesthesia, incisions, ionizing radiation, or contrast agents.
- Produces predictable, bounded adverse events. The realistic worst-case outcome from an incorrectly prescribed pelvic floor exercise program is that it does not improve symptoms, or the participant experiences mild and self-limiting muscle soreness. This is categorically different from AI systems recommending medication dosages or surgical approaches where errors can produce irreversible harm.

The exercises prescribed -- Kegel contractions, diaphragmatic breathing, bridges, lifestyle modifications -- are widely recommended as self-care by ACOG, the International Continence Society, and the Academy of Pelvic Health Physical Therapy. The AI-supported workflow adds clinical structure, personalization, and oversight to exercises that millions of women already perform unsupervised.

1. Prevention-First Safety Architecture

The pilot is structured to reduce risk before remediation is needed:
- 100% clinician review of care plans before delivery. A licensed physical therapist reviews and approves every participant-facing plan using a standardized checklist. The reviewing clinician may modify any element. Average review time: 3--5 minutes per plan.
- Upfront clinical governance. A Board-Certified Women's Health Clinical Specialist (WCS) authored or reviewed the scoring algorithms, exercise library, escalation logic, and plan generation rules based on published evidence.
- Red-flag screening with immediate routing. Reported symptoms indicating urgent or emergent conditions are routed out of the platform to emergency or in-person medical care. No exercise plan is generated. Additionally, a safety gate mechanism tracks any subsequent attempt to change a positive safety answer, requiring explicit patient confirmation and logging the change as a SAFETY_ANSWER_CHANGED event for PT review and OAIP oversight.
- Standardized clinician review checklist. Documented separately (see Appendix D).
- Tiered escalation logic. Standard pathway, enhanced monitoring (ICIQ >=13, high avoidance, medication modification), and clinical referral (ICIQ >=19, complex presentation) based on validated severity scores and behavioral indicators.
- Pudendal neuralgia detection. The system flags suspected pudendal neuralgia when a patient reports sitting-related pain triggers combined with composite pain >6/10. This triggers ICD-10 code G57.91, a precaution against prolonged sitting exercises, and a mandatory PT review guardrail alert. The PT evaluates nerve involvement before progressing the exercise program.
- Prenatal exercise safety protocol. Pregnant patients receive prenatal-adapted care (see Section 3.8, Prenatal Pelvic Floor Protocol).
- Full audit logging. AI-generated draft outputs, clinician review actions (approve/modify/reject), timestamps, and participant-facing final plans are logged and available for regulatory review.

This prevention-first design materially lowers the expected corrective care frequency.

3. Root-Cause Classification

Reported concerns will be classified using a defined framework that adapts to the pilot phase:
- Phase 1 (Calibration -- clinician-reviewed): Every plan is reviewed by a licensed clinician before delivery. Review considers the AI-generated draft, the clinician's edits/approvals, and participant context.
- Phase 2/3 (Graduated autonomy): For auto-approved plans, analysis distinguishes between Protocol Adherence (the system applied the validated rules as designed, but the outcome was suboptimal) and Algorithmic Deviation (the system did not execute the rules/exclusions as designed). For clinician-reviewed plans, the Phase 1 framework applies.

Classification categories:
- AI/System Deviation: The platform failed to apply validated scoring logic, exclusions, or routing correctly (e.g., missed a red-flag criterion, selected an exercise outside the severity tier).
- Clinical Review Miss: A clinician-reviewed plan contained an issue that was not identified or corrected during review (Phase 1 clinician-reviewed plans only).
- Protocol Limitation (Needs Refinement): The system followed the validated protocol, but pilot learnings indicate the protocol needs adjustment for certain presentations.
- Participant Execution/Adherence: Non-adherence, misunderstanding, or deviation from instructions contributed to the issue.
- Indeterminate/Multifactorial: No single cause can be identified.

This classification supports safety review, quality improvement, and pilot reporting -- not assignment of legal liability.

4. Corrective Care Commitment

Step 3: Scope-Defined Corrective Care

If Expect's clinical review determines that corrective care is clinically appropriate and the issue was likely attributable (in whole or in part) to the AI-supported workflow and/or its clinical oversight process, Expect will provide:
- 1 expedited clinical review

- Up to 1 corrective telehealth PT session
- Up to 2 brief follow-up monitoring touchpoints
- Referral support for in-person pelvic floor PT evaluation, where clinically indicated and reasonably available

Corrective care is defined by scope of services, not participant-facing dollar caps.

Given the low-risk intervention profile (Section 0), the most likely corrective action is plan revision and additional clinician monitoring -- not emergency intervention or specialist referral.

APPENDIX C: Data Governance, De-Identification, and Research Framework

Expect -- Utah OAIP Pilot -- February 2026

1. Overview
This appendix describes how Expect collects, stores, de-identifies, and shares data generated through the Utah OAIP pilot. It addresses the four data-sharing commitments in the participant consent form: (1) Utah OAIP oversight, (2) IRB-approved research, (3) external auditors, and (4) anonymized public dashboards.

2. Data Collected
The platform collects the following categories of data during the pilot:
- Clinical intake data: Responses to validated instruments (ICIQ-UI SF, selected FLUTS Filling/Voiding items, ICIQ-FLUTSsex, GUPI-Female, PHQ-2, Bristol Stool Scale, POPDI-6, and NRS pain scales), symptom history, medication history, activity avoidance, cue preference, pregnancy status, delivery date and delivery type (for postpartum patients, used to compute weeks postpartum for confound control), bowel function, and patient goals. All self-reported.
- Demographic data: Name, date of birth, contact information (for clinical communication only), referring provider.
- Treatment plan data: AI-generated draft plans, clinician-reviewed final plans, exercise prescriptions, adjunct recommendations, diagnostic codes, CPT codes.
- Clinical governance data: Clinician review timestamps, review duration, structured PT modification diffs (exercise adds/removes/parameter changes, adjunct changes, goal modifications), approval/rejection decisions, clinical notes.

- Audit data: Complete log of system interactions, AI outputs, clinician actions, and participant-facing deliverables.

3. Data Storage and Security
- HIPAA compliance: All individually identifiable health information (IIHI) is stored in Convex HIPAA-compliant cloud infrastructure (Enterprise Plan, SOC 2 Type II audited, BAA executed) with automatic encryption at rest and in transit. Patient records, audit events, outcome records, and session data persist across sessions in Convex tables with function-level access controls. The consent form transparently discloses the data storage approach.
- Access controls: The platform implements role-based view separation with three distinct views (Patient, PT Provider, OAIP), enforced by server-side authentication and authorization. PT providers authenticate with individual email and PBKDF2-hashed password (600,000 iterations, per OWASP 2023 recommendation). OAIP reviewers authenticate with a shared access code verified server-side (fail-closed if not configured). All authenticated sessions use cryptographic tokens with 30-minute expiry and automatic timeout with 60-second warning. The API proxy enforces role-based function access (PT-only, OAIP-only, patient-self) on every request. Auditor Mode de-identification is available for regulatory review (see Section 4.3).
- Retention: Clinical records retained per Utah and federal healthcare record retention requirements (minimum 7 years). Pilot-specific data retained for the duration of the OAIP agreement plus 3 years for regulatory reporting.
- Cyber liability coverage: $1,000,000 minimum (see Appendix B, Section 7).

4. De-Identification Standard
All data shared outside the clinical treatment relationship is de-identified using the HIPAA Safe Harbor method (45 CFR 164.514(b)(2)), which requires removal of 18 specified identifiers:
Names, geographic data smaller than state, dates (except year) for individuals over 89, phone/fax numbers, email addresses, SSN, medical record numbers, health plan beneficiary numbers, account numbers, certificate/license numbers, vehicle identifiers, device identifiers, web URLs, IP addresses, biometric identifiers, full-face photographs, and any other unique identifying number or code.

De-identification is performed programmatically before any data export. The de-identification process is documented and auditable.

Note: Auditor Mode (see Section 4.3) provides display-level de-identification for real-time regulatory review, distinct from the full HIPAA Safe Harbor de-identification applied to research datasets and public reporting exports.

APPENDIX D (Updated): Clinical Reviewer Checklist -- Utah OAIP Pilot

Dr. Nicole Dugan, PT, DPT, WCS -- 3-5 Minute Review Guide

The system handles scoring and exercise selection automatically. Your role is to verify logic matched correctly and no edge cases slipped through. Keep reviews under 5 minutes.

Step 1: Safety Verification
- Confirm no 'Yes' answers for red flags (bleeding, fever, chest pain, headache, UTI). System should have redirected -- quick visual check for glitches.
- If a safety answer was changed during intake, review the SAFETY_ANSWER_CHANGED alert and verify the patient's revised answer is clinically appropriate.

Step 2: Tier Assignment
- Check ICIQ Total and verify correct tier:
  - ICIQ 13-21 -> Beginner: 12 weeks, starts with Supine PF Activation, PT review q3 days
  - ICIQ 6-12 -> Moderate: 8 weeks, standard Quick Squeeze start, weekly check-in
  - ICIQ 1-5 -> Advanced: 6 weeks, higher volume, functional positions, bi-weekly check-in

Step 3: Adjunct Verification
- Pain >= 4 -> Pain De-Sensitization Breathing should be first exercise
- Pain >= 5 OR Dyspareunia -> Vaginal Dilator Therapy adjunct
- Urgency or Mixed subtype -> Bladder Retraining adjunct
- Constipation >= 2 -> Bowel Management Program
- Medication modification = Yes -> Medication Review Referral adjunct with DIAPPERS framework note

Step 4: Cue Personalization Check
- Confirm exercise instructions use the patient's selected cue style (biologic / imaginative / breathing / simple contract / default)
- Instructions should NOT say only 'squeeze your pelvic floor' or 'engage your pelvic floor' -- research shows these general cues are least effective (Crane & Dugan, 2025)
- If biologic cue selected: verify the urination warning appears ("Never practice this during actual urination")

Step 5: Avoidance and Escalation Check
- If >= 3 avoided activities: verify HIGH IMPACT flag is set and goal includes resuming avoided activities
- If ICIQ >= 19 OR (>= 3 avoided activities + elevated pain): verify enhanced monitoring is flagged
- If medication modification = Yes: verify Medication Review Referral adjunct is present
- If pudendal neuralgia indicators present (sitting_long trigger + pain >6/10): verify G57.91 diagnosis and "avoid prolonged sitting exercises" precaution are present

Step 5b: Prenatal Protocol Check (if applicable)
- If patient indicated active pregnancy: verify PRENATAL_FLAG is set and prenatal exercise substitutions have been applied
- Confirm incline/side-lying variants are appropriate for the patient's current trimester
- Verify the vena cava compression precaution block is present
- Confirm the care plan is labeled "Prenatal Pelvic Floor Protocol"

Step 6: Routing Destination Check
- Verify provider fax verification status (VERIFIED / DEFAULT / MANUAL ENTRY)
- If DEFAULT: confirm the pre-populated fax number is correct for the provider's organization and specialty
- If concierge pending: confirm transmission hold is active

Step 7: Final Sign-Off
- Does the overall plan make clinical sense for this patient's presentation?
- Any red flags the system may have missed?
- Are exercise parameters (sets, reps, hold times) appropriate for the assigned tier?

If everything checks out: Click 'Approve Plan.' Target review time: 3-5 minutes.
If something needs adjustment: Modify the plan directly in the review interface. All modifications are logged in the audit trail.

This checklist corresponds to the prevention-first safety architecture described in Appendix B, Section 1.


APPENDIX E: Operational Design, Pilot Phases, and Patient Experience

Expect -- Utah OAIP Pilot -- February 2026

1. The Access Gap in Utah

As described in Section 1.3, Utah has approximately 7 pelvic floor PT specialists for 3.4 million total residents (approximately 1.6 million adult women). Wait times average 6-12 weeks in urban areas; rural access is essentially unavailable. For many women, this pilot may be the only way they receive evidence-based pelvic floor care.

2. Clinical Evidence Base

The Expect protocol uses validated clinical instruments, peer-reviewed evidence, and a licensed clinician review layer.

2a. Validated Instruments
- ICIQ-UI SF (International Consultation on Incontinence Questionnaire, Short Form): The gold standard for measuring urinary incontinence severity. Validated, widely used in clinical trials and clinical practice globally. Administered in full.
- FLUTS Filling/Voiding items (selected from ICIQ-FLUTS): Nocturia, urgency, daytime frequency (Filling) and hesitancy, straining, intermittency (Voiding) with bother scores. The FLUTS Incontinence subscale is omitted because the ICIQ-UI SF already captures incontinence severity and subtype.
- ICIQ-FLUTSsex: Sexual health impact assessment. Administered in full.
- GUPI-Female (Genitourinary Pain Index): Validated pain assessment for pelvic pain conditions.
- PHQ-2: Two-item validated depression screen.
- POPDI-6 (Pelvic Organ Prolapse Distress Inventory, short form): Six-item symptom-based prolapse screening with conditional bother scale.

2b. Evidence-Based Exercise Selection
The specific exercises in the Expect protocol (Bridge Lift, Quick Squeeze Kegels, Long Hold Kegels, Diaphragmatic Breathing) are directly linked to ICIQ score reduction through pelvic floor strengthening. Prenatal patients receive pregnancy-safe exercise substitutions (see Section 3.8). The protocol uses the validated "Imaginative Cueing" framework from Crane, Dugan et al. (2025), co-authored by Expect's Doctor of Physical Therapy. This paper is one of the most downloaded in the history of the Journal of Women's & Pelvic Health Physical Therapy. The framework statistically improves neuromuscular recruitment (P < .001).

2c. ICIQ Minimal Clinically Important Difference (MCID)
The pilot success metric of ICIQ improvement >= 3 points average exceeds the Minimal Clinically Important Difference (MCID) of 2.5 points for the ICIQ-UI SF. This means the pilot is not looking for "better numbers"--it is looking for clinically significant improvement that patients can feel.

3. Pilot Phases and PT Review Evolution

See Table 2 (Section 3.1) for complete phase definitions and benchmarks. In summary:
- Phase 1 (Months 1-4): 100% PT review of all AI-generated plans. Target review time: 3-5 minutes. PT may modify, reject, or rebuild any plan.
- Phase 2 (Months 5-8): Risk-stratified review. Low-complexity cases may receive expedited review; high-complexity cases remain 100% PT review.
- Phase 3 (Months 9-12): Low-complexity plans may be auto-approved with retrospective audit. High-complexity cases continue prospective PT review.

Phase transitions require OAIP notification and approval. Any adverse event or safety concern can trigger a return to Phase 1.

4. Success and Failure Definitions

Primary success metric: Mean ICIQ-UI SF score improvement >= 3 points across the pilot cohort (exceeds MCID of 2.5 points).

Secondary metrics:
- Patient satisfaction score >= 4.0/5.0
- Protocol adherence rate >= 60%
- Clinician modification rate < 10% (Phase 1 benchmark)
- Zero unresolved adverse events
- Pain score reduction in patients with baseline pain >= 3/10

Failure triggers (any one triggers review):
- Adverse event rate > 2% of participants
- Clinician modification rate > 20% (indicates AI is generating poor plans)
- Mean ICIQ improvement < 2 points at 8-week mark
- Patient dropout rate > 40% before 4-week mark
- Any serious adverse event attributable to AI-supported workflow

Termination triggers:
- Any participant harm directly attributable to AI error not caught by PT review
- Loss of clinical oversight (PT unavailability exceeding 48 hours)
- OAIP directive to terminate
- Loss of insurance coverage

6. Vulnerable Populations Considerations

The Expect pilot addresses multiple vulnerable populations:
- Currently pregnant patients: Conditional screening for high-risk pregnancy. Normal pregnancies receive a prenatal-adapted care plan (see Section 3.8, Prenatal Pelvic Floor Protocol).
- Postpartum patients: Safety screening includes postpartum-specific red flags (fever, headache with vision changes). Warning messages explicitly note postpartum urgency while remaining applicable to non-postpartum patients.
- Rural Utah residents: As noted in Section 1.3, rural Utahns have effectively no access to pelvic floor PT. This pilot may be their only access point.
- Patients with pain: The GUPI instrument and NRS pain scales screen for significant pain. The exercise protocol deprioritizes strength and prioritizes pain management for patients with pain scores >= 4/10. Pain De-Sensitization Breathing is placed first in their program.
- Patients modifying medications: Patients who report changing prescribed medication due to urinary symptoms receive a Medication Review Referral adjunct with DIAPPERS framework guidance.
- Patients with high avoidance: Patients avoiding 3+ activity categories receive HIGH IMPACT classification with enhanced monitoring and functional goal-setting.
- Low health literacy: The patient-facing care plan uses plain language, visual scores, expandable exercise instructions with step-by-step guidance, common mistake warnings, and stop conditions.
- Patients in crisis: Red-flag screening redirects to 911 or physician immediately (see Section 1.4, Step 2).
- Patients with suspected pudendal neuralgia: Sitting-related pain triggers + pain >6/10 flagged for PT review with ICD-10 G57.91 (see Section 1.4, Step 5).
